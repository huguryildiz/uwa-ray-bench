Metric glossary

How each scorecard column is defined and scored vs BELLHOP3D

T^i = model TL · Ti = BELLHOP3D TL, per grid cell i (N=101·49·31 cells) · R = receiver · shadow cutoff 120 dB.

TL RMSEfull-field error · dB
1N ∑i=1N (T^i−Ti)2

Root-mean-square gap between the model's transmission-loss field and BELLHOP3D over every cell of the 101×49×31 grid, shadow zones included. Lower means the whole field tracks ground truth.

Core RMSEinsonified error · dB leader
1|C| ∑i∈C (T^i−Ti)2

The same RMSE but only over cells insonified in both fields — C={i:T^i,Ti<120} dB — so empty shadow zones can't flatter the score. This is the leaderboard metric; lowest wins.

TL(R)receiver loss · dB
TL(R)=−20 log10 |p(R)|pref

The transmission loss in dB at the single receiver point R each panel reports. It anchors agreement at the one scored operating point (canonical 41×31).

TL(R) errreceiver error · dB
ΔTL(R)=| TLmdl(R)− TLref(R)|

Absolute deviation of the model's receiver TL from BELLHOP3D's. Isolates accuracy at the target point, independent of the rest of the field.

Recipself-consistency · dB
εrecip=| TL(S→R)− TL(R→S)|

In a reciprocal medium, source→receiver and receiver→source loss must match. This residual is a physics self-consistency check that should sit near 0 dB.

Conv ΔTL(R)convergence · dB
Δconv=| TL2n(R)− TLn(R)|

How much the receiver TL shifts when the ray fan is refined (count n→2n). A small value means the solution has converged, not an artifact of under-sampling.

Insonif%coverage · %
|{i:TLi<120}| N×100

Share of grid cells the model marks as insonified (TL below the 120 dB shadow cutoff) — how much of the volume the field reaches versus shadow.

|Δy| 3Dout-of-plane · km
|Δy|= maxray |yray−y0|

Peak lateral excursion a ray makes out of its launch vertical plane — a genuinely 3D effect from horizontal refraction and bathymetry. A bonus/tiebreaker axis, not core score.

FPSrender rate · fps
FPS=NframesΔt

The panel's live render throughput — not physics, but a read on whether the dense ~500k-segment ray fan holds an interactive frame rate.

BELLHOP3D Physics

Governing equations for 3D underwater acoustic ray propagation · scenario parameters

Eikonal Equationwave-front · ray basis foundation
|∇τ|2 = 1c2(x)

Foundation of ray acoustics: τ(x) is the travel-time field. Its gradient p = ∇τ is the slowness vector (|p| = 1/c), perpendicular to wavefronts and tangent to rays. Solving the eikonal fully determines all ray paths.

Ray EquationsHamiltonian · arc length s
dxds = c(x)·p dpds = −∇(1c)

Hamilton's equations in 3D. x(s) traces the ray position; p(s) = ∇τ the slowness vector (|p| = 1/c). Integrated forward along arc length s from the source with initial azimuth θaz and elevation θel launch angles.

Snell's Lawinterface BC · refraction
sin⁡θ1c1 = sin⁡θ2c2 = const

The tangential component of slowness p is conserved at every interface. In a continuously stratified medium this bends rays toward lower-c regions (Fermat's principle), generating the deep SOFAR channel. In 3D it also causes horizontal (azimuthal) deflection over sloped bathymetry.

Geometric Spreadingamplitude · Jacobian
A(s) = A0 c0J0 c(s)·J(s)

Pressure amplitude along the ray via energy conservation in the ray tube. J(s) = |∂(x,y)/∂(q₁,q₂)| is the Jacobian of the ray mapping from launch angles q to spatial position. J → 0 at caustics where amplitude diverges.

Pressure FieldWKBJ ray solution
p(x,ω) = ∑k Ak(x) · eiωτk(x)

WKBJ approximation to the Helmholtz equation. Each ray arrival k contributes an amplitude A_k and phase ωτ_k; arrivals are summed coherently. BELLHOP's Gaussian-beam variant replaces the Dirac-delta spreading with a finite-width Gaussian to avoid caustic singularities.

Sound Speed Profilec(z) · m s⁻¹ · scenario
c(z)= { 1520−0.10·z z≤200 m 1500+0.018(z−200) z>200 m

Bi-linear profile: −10 m s⁻¹ km⁻¹ negative gradient in the mixed layer (0–200 m) producing a near-surface shadow zone; then +18 m s⁻¹ km⁻¹ positive gradient below, forming a deep SOFAR (Sound Fixing and Ranging) channel axis.

BathymetryD(x,y) · m · scenario
D=2500 − ∑k=12 Hk exp(−rk2) rk2 = (x−xkak)2 + (y−ykbk)2

Two Gaussian seamounts on a 2500 m flat basin. k=1: H=1500 m, (x₁,y₁)=(18,12) km, (a,b)=(2.5,3.0) km. k=2: H=1900 m, (x₂,y₂)=(34,9) km, (a,b)=(2.0,2.5) km. Sloped flanks generate horizontal refraction — the primary 3D out-of-plane deflection effect in this scenario.
